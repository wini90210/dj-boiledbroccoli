Politics is going digital - but how much does it really matter?

Photo courtesy: Wikipedia

Published on August 9, 2024

By Manaswini Pillai

Politics - and politicians - do not change much, even when they do change. However, one aspect that has changed is the way of reaching the masses.

Digital and social media advertising have taken centre stage in political campaigning. Google Ads, including Google Maps, Google Play, Google Shopping, Google Search and YouTube, still remain the most popular form of digital advertising.

Facebook and Instagram trail close behind Google for political advertising. The Trump campaign also spent $1.2M more on Facebook and Instagram ads and made 817M more audience impressions than the Biden campaign, but the federal 2020 election results were in the latter’s favour.

But does digital advertising expenditure impact electoral outcomes at all? Jennifer Stromer-Galley, author of Presidential Campaigning in the Internet Age, does not think so. “Digital advertising is not a strong predictor of electoral outcomes,” she said. “There is no good evidence that ad spending leads to winning elections.”

And to corroborate her claim, in 2020, the Trump campaign spent more than the Biden campaign on Google Ads in states like California, Georgia, New Mexico, Oregon, Washington, Arizona and New York and still lost those states.

Moreover, according to a report by Statista, political ad spending shows no signs of slowing down. The report projects an approximate growth of 14% in ad spending in the 2024 election cycle as compared to 2020.

Another report by Statista also surveyed eligible voters in the United States in 2022, and over 75 percent reported they “tend to tune out or ignore political ads”. But affecting consumer behaviour or not, digital advertising still remains one of the biggest spends in political campaigns - and it isn’t going away anytime soon.
